# Portfolio framing guide

Your portfolio lives at andrearobin.com. This guide tells you how to frame and present it in each sector context — what to lead with, what to emphasize, and what questions each case study answers for that audience.

---

## Your three case studies and their strategic value

### Case study 1: Province-wide transition to care-based service
**Sector tags:** Public sector · organizational transformation · disability case management

| Sector | What this case demonstrates | How to introduce it |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| BC public sector | You can work at scale, navigate complex stakeholder environments, and design learning that supports genuine organizational change — not just compliance | "I've designed learning for a province-wide workforce transition — [X] staff across multiple regions, tight timeline, high stakes for service recipients." |
| Research | You understand how to connect learning design to a theory of change, and how to evaluate whether it worked | "This project required building an evaluation framework before the curriculum existed — a logic model approach that connected learning outcomes to service delivery outcomes." |
| Higher ed | Less directly relevant — use sparingly; frame around the equity angle | "The disability case management context required genuine accessibility practice — design for the full range of learners, not the imagined average." |

### Case study 2: Learning evaluation framework for a national research institution
**Sector tags:** Research · curriculum design · inclusive facilitation

| Sector | What this case demonstrates | How to introduce it |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Research institutions | You understand research culture, can build rigorous evaluation frameworks, and can work with a technically sophisticated audience | "I built a learning evaluation framework from scratch for a national research institution — starting with a theory of change and working backward to curriculum design." |
| Higher ed | You can design programs that are coherent, assessed, and evidence-based — not just content-delivered | "This project required connecting learning outcomes to institutional outcomes, which is exactly the constructive alignment challenge most university programs face." |
| Public sector | You can produce frameworks that organizations can own and continue using after you've left | "The deliverable was designed for sustainability — built to be used by internal staff, not dependent on my continued involvement." |

### Case study 3: Equitable experiential learning at scale
**Sector tags:** Higher education · program design · accessibility

| Sector | What this case demonstrates | How to introduce it |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Higher ed | You can design equitable, accessible programs at scale, work within academic structures, and address real barriers to participation | "This project involved redesigning an experiential learning program so it worked for students who had historically been excluded — students with disabilities, students with caregiving responsibilities, students from equity-deserving communities." |
| Cultural orgs | You understand informal and non-traditional learning contexts and how to design for diverse learners | "The challenge was making experiential learning genuinely accessible, not just technically compliant — which required rethinking the design premises, not just adding accommodations." |

## Fourth case study — in development

**Recommended focus:** Facilitation and capacity building — particularly the wellness liaison work and stakeholder co-design processes

**Why this case will strengthen the portfolio:** Your current three cases are strong on systems design and evaluation. They are somewhat light on the relational, facilitative dimensions of your practice — the psychological safety work, the co-design processes, the capacity building that happens when you work alongside people rather than designing for them. A fourth case that foregrounds this would:

- Round out the picture of your practice
- Speak directly to cultural org and higher ed audiences who care deeply about facilitation
- Demonstrate the "Relationships" work value in practice, not just positioning

**Possible sources for this case:**
- A workshop or facilitation series where the design of the container was as important as the content
- A stakeholder co-design process with significant complexity
- A wellness liaison engagement that produced measurable outcomes
- A capacity-building project where your goal was to make yourself unnecessary

## How to reference the portfolio in applications

**In cover letters:** "Case studies from higher education, research, and public sector contexts are available at andrearobin.com."

**In interview prep emails:** "I wanted to flag that the [Case Study X] might be particularly relevant to our conversation — it covers [brief description]. Happy to discuss it in detail."

**When asked "do you have a portfolio?":** "Yes — andrearobin.com has three case studies, all anonymized in accordance with confidentiality obligations. I'm also happy to discuss the work in more detail in conversation, which often gives a fuller picture than the written cases alone."

**If they ask about the anonymization:** "All projects are described in a way that protects organizational confidentiality while conveying the nature, scope, and methodology of the work. If there's specific context that would be useful, I'm happy to discuss the work directly."

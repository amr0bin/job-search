# Offer evaluation framework

When an offer comes, you'll be excited and possibly under time pressure. This framework slows that down.
Never accept or decline in the room. "I'm very interested — can I have 48 hours to review the full details?"

---

## Must-have checklist

Before evaluating anything else, confirm all of these:

- [ ] Remote or hybrid work arrangement confirmed in writing
- [ ] Base salary at or above $100,000
- [ ] Extended health benefits (dental, vision, paramedical)
- [ ] Vancouver, BC or fully remote Canada
- [ ] Public or social good mission (confirmed, not just stated)
- [ ] Role scoped at design/specialist level (not coordinator execution)

**If any of these is missing:** negotiate before accepting. If non-negotiable and absent, this is a no.

## Questions to ask before accepting

- Can you confirm the remote/hybrid arrangement in the offer letter?
- What does the professional development budget cover, and how is it accessed?
- What are the step increase timelines and criteria?
- Is this a term or permanent position?
- What's the probationary period and what does it entail?
- When would you need a decision by?

---

## Negotiation notes

**Always negotiate.** The worst outcome is they say no. The most common outcome is movement.

**What's negotiable even in "fixed" salary environments:**
- Starting step within the band
- Additional vacation days
- Remote work arrangement formalized in writing
- Professional development budget
- Start date (more time = more leverage to finish on good terms at current role)

**Script for counter-offer:**
> "Thank you so much for this offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. I'd like to discuss the compensation. Based on my experience and the scope of the position, I was hoping we could look at [specific ask]. Is there flexibility there?"

---

## The gut check

After all the analysis, ask yourself:

1. When I imagine starting this job in three months, how do I feel?
2. Does the person I'd be reporting to seem like someone I can learn from and be respected by?
3. Would I be proud to tell people where I work and what I do?
4. If the salary were 10% lower, would I still take it?

If the answers are mostly yes: take it.
If there's persistent hesitation that the numbers don't explain: trust it.

# Warm outreach strategy

The research is consistent: most professional roles — especially at the senior/specialist level — are filled through networks before or during the posting period. Outreach is not optional. It is the primary channel.

---

## The mindset shift

You are not asking for favours. You are:
- Letting people who respect your work know you're available
- Giving people the chance to connect you with opportunities they may already know about
- Building relationships that will matter beyond this search

Most people genuinely like helping someone they respect find a good role. Make it easy for them to do that.

---

## The outreach tiers

### Tier 1 — Former colleagues and direct collaborators
People who have worked alongside you and seen your work firsthand. These conversations are easiest and most likely to produce warm leads.

**Goal:** A 20-minute call. Not to ask for a job — to ask what they're seeing in the sector and whether anyone comes to mind.

**Target:** 8–10 people. One conversation per week.

### Tier 2 — Adjacent contacts
People you've met professionally, share a field with, or have a mutual connection to. Weaker ties, but often more useful than strong ties for job searching (Granovetter's weak ties research).

**Goal:** A brief email exchange or 20-minute call. Wider net.

**Target:** 5–8 people over the course of the search.

### Tier 3 — Strategic cold outreach
Specific hiring managers or team leads at Tier 1 target orgs where you have no warm connection. Done only after you've researched the org thoroughly.

**Goal:** To get on someone's radar before a posting appears. Even a "thanks for reaching out, we'll keep you in mind" is a win.

**Target:** 1–2 per month, maximum. Quality over quantity.

## Outreach cadence

| Week | Actions |
|------|---------|
| Week 1 | Draft your contact map. Identify 3 Tier 1 people. Send first 2 messages. |
| Week 2 | Follow up on week 1. Send 2 more. Have first conversation. |
| Week 3 | Continue. Begin Tier 2 outreach. |
| Week 4 | Review: who responded? Who led somewhere? Adjust focus. |
| Ongoing | 2–3 new outreach messages per week until you're in active interview stage. |

---

## What to do in the conversation

A 20-minute informational call has a simple structure:

1. **Thank them + set the frame** (2 min): "I really appreciate you making time. I'm doing a deliberate search and trying to understand the landscape better before I go deep on applications."

2. **Ask about their work** (5 min): "What are you working on these days?" People like talking about their work. This is not small talk — it's intelligence.

3. **Share your positioning** (3 min): "I'm looking for a learning design or L&D role in [sectors], ideally remote/hybrid in Vancouver at the senior/specialist level. Here's what I bring that's a bit different: [your positioning statement, brief]."

4. **Ask the two key questions** (8 min):
   - "What's your read on where the interesting L&D work is happening in [sector] right now?"
   - "Is there anyone you'd suggest I talk to — either at your org or elsewhere?"

5. **Close gracefully** (2 min): "This was really helpful. Would it be okay if I followed up if I have specific questions as the search develops?"
